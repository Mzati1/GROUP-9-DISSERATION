\section{Background}

Digital forensics involves the scientific investigation of crimes committed through digital systems, focusing on the identification, acquisition, preservation, and analysis of digital evidence \citep{casey2014}. It plays a vital role in law enforcement and cybersecurity, ensuring that digital evidence is admissible in court. The growing complexity of cybercrimes such as fraud, impersonation, and unauthorised access has led to the development of specialised forensic tools that maintain the integrity of evidence during investigations \citep{karie2015}.

Recent global reports highlight a surge in cybercrimes involving messaging platforms, particularly WhatsApp, which has become one of the most exploited channels for online fraud. In early 2024, over 43,000 cyber fraud cases in India were linked to WhatsApp, including phishing, impersonation, and investment scams \citep{ministryhomeaffairs2024}. Meta also reported removing 6.8 million scam accounts in 2025, many operated by organised cybercrime groups in Southeast Asia that use WhatsApp, Telegram, and TikTok to coordinate large scale fraud operations \citep{ikeda2025}. These networks often lure users into fake investment or cryptocurrency groups, revealing how WhatsApp has evolved into a global hub for cybercriminal collaboration and conspiracy.

In Malawi, mobile-based scams and other cybercrimes are also on the rise \citep{nyirenda2024}. Although Section 83 of the Electronic Transactions and Cyber Security Act No.\ 33 of 2016 provides legal authority for the seizure and preservation of digital evidence, law enforcement agencies continue to face challenges in dealing with encrypted communication platforms like WhatsApp.

WhatsApp is a widely used instant messaging and voice-over-IP application owned by Meta \cite{whatsapp2026}. It enables text messaging, voice and video calls, and the sharing of multimedia files and documents. A defining feature is its default end-to-end encryption, which ensures that only the communicating users can read messages, making third-party interception extremely difficult. Available on mobile and desktop platforms, WhatsApp requires a phone number for registration and supports group chats, broadcast lists, and status updates, which has made it both a popular communication tool and a frequent vector for cyber-enabled crimes.

Existing forensic tools are often general-purpose and struggle with WhatsApp-specific challenges such as end-to-end encryption, data volatility, and evolving storage formats. These limitations reduce their reliability in evidence acquisition and analysis \citep{vinayagam2025}.



% ------ Section 1.2 ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Problem Statement}

Although Malawi has laws against cyber offences such as cyberstalking, fraud, impersonation, and data breaches, enforcing these laws and successfully prosecuting offenders remains a major challenge\cite{nyirenda2024, kajoloweka2022}. This difficulty is compounded by the growing number of cybercrime cases linked to WhatsApp, the country's most widely used communication platform \citep{chiputula2024}. Increasing incidents of mobile money fraud, fake social media accounts, and cyberbullying highlight the urgent need to enhance existing digital forensic tools with WhatsApp-specific capabilities to better support law enforcement efforts and improve the effectiveness of cybercrime investigations in Malawi \citep{balogh2025}. The main challenge is the limitations of existing digital forensic tools and methodologies, which affect the efficient investigation of WhatsApp-related cybercrime. These limitations manifest in several key areas \citep{oxygen2023, sutikno2024}.

Firstly, there is no open-source forensic toolkit that offers a unified, automated framework for decrypting, parsing, and visualising WhatsApp data across its varied encrypted formats, such as crypt12, crypt14, and existing formats. Existing tools like WhatsApp Viewer, Whapa, and WhatsApp Key/Database Extractor perform isolated functions and lack interoperability, forcing investigators to manually chain disjointed processes, often requiring unreliable workarounds like application downgrading for key extraction, decryption, and analysis. This fragmented workflow is time-consuming, error-prone, and inconsistent across backup versions and container structures \citep{tusharanshu2023}.

Secondly, conventional digital forensic acquisition techniques remain heavily reliant on full-device imaging, a process designed for broad data capture rather than focusing on application-level acquisition. This outdated approach is misaligned with modern investigative needs, where the primary evidence often resides within specific platforms such as WhatsApp. Full-device imaging generates excessive and largely irrelevant data, consumes significant storage and processing resources, and prolongs examination timelines. For investigators operating under limited technical capacity and time constraints, this results in unnecessary workload, slower case resolution, and a diminished ability to respond effectively to rapidly evolving cyber incidents \citep{aggarwal2019}.

\section{Introduction}

This chapter presents a comprehensive review of existing relevant literature on digital forensics, WhatsApp forensic analysis, and mobile cybercrime investigations, with specific attention to the Malawian context. The review is organised into five major areas: the global perspective of WhatsApp forensicgs, the local Malawian perspective and legal framework, stakeholder challenges, identified research gaps, and the theoretical and technical foundations that inform the design of the proposed unified WhatsApp forensic toolkit. The chapter examines current forensic tools, techniques, and challenges associated with WhatsApp data acquisition and analysis.

% ------ Section 2.2 --------------------------------------------
\section{Global Perspective}

WhatsApp has grown to become the world's most widely used instant messaging platform, with over two billion registered monthly active users as of 2022 \citep{nishchal2024}. Its ubiquity in personal, professional, and increasingly criminal communication has made it a central artefact in digital forensic investigations globally. Internationally, the development of WhatsApp forensic methods has evolved significantly over the past decade, progressing from basic manual extraction to sophisticated automated acquisition procedures capable of handling encrypted databases, multimedia evidence, and deleted artefact recovery. 
\citep{anglano2014, prabowo2025}.

Commercial forensic platforms have led this evolution. Tools such as Cellebrite UFED and Oxygen Forensic Detective offer comprehensive acquisition capabilities for WhatsApp artefacts, including message databases, encryption keys, media files, and cloud backups for both Android and iOS devices \citep{nishchal2024}. These platforms enable investigators to perform full-device imaging, logical extraction, and application-level targeted acquisition, each method offering different trade-offs between comprehensiveness, speed, and investigative relevance \citep{sutikno2024, oxygen2023}.

However, scholars such as \citet{nishchal2024} noted that traditional whole-device imaging is often inefficient when only specific application data (e.g., chats, media, contacts) are required. The indiscriminate capture of all device data not only consumes excessive storage and processing resources but also prolongs examination timelines and introduces risks of data contamination. \citet{aggarwal2019} further argue that targeted, application-level data extraction tools represent a more appropriate and efficient paradigm for modern mobile investigations, particularly in resource-constrained environments. Consequently, specialised tools and methods that enable application-specific data extraction are generally preferable.

\emph{The technical architecture of WhatsApp on Android stores communication artefacts across two primary SQLite database files: \texttt{msgstore.db}, which contains all message records, and \texttt{wa.db}, which holds contact identity information. The \texttt{msgstore.db} database contains tables including \texttt{message}, \texttt{chat}, \texttt{jid}, and \texttt{message\_media}, which together enable reconstruction of complete communication histories, participant identities, timestamps, and multimedia evidence trails. The \texttt{wa.db} database contains a \texttt{wa\_contacts} table that resolves internal WhatsApp identifiers ( Jabber IDs, also called JID's) to real-world phone numbers and contact names, forming a critical cross-reference for identity attribution during investigations.}

Despite advancements in targeted extraction techniques, significant challenges persist globally. \citet{tusharanshu2023} identify the fragmentation of open-source forensic tools as a major barrier to effective WhatsApp investigations. Existing open source tools such as WhatsApp Viewer, Whapa, and WhatsApp Key/Database Extractor each address isolated aspects of the forensic workflow but lack interoperability \citep{tusharanshu2023}. Investigators must manually chain these disjointed processes, often resorting to unreliable workarounds such as application downgrading to extract encryption keys, resulting in workflows that are time consuming, error-prone, and inconsistent across backup versions and database schemas \citep{nishchal2024, tusharanshu2023}.

The encryption architecture of WhatsApp further compounds forensic challenges. WhatsApp employs end-to-end encryption for all communications using the Signal Protocol, ensuring that messages cannot be intercepted in transit \citep{vijayan2018, nishchal2024}. On Android devices, database backups are stored in \texttt{.crypt12}, \texttt{.crypt14}, or \texttt{.crypt15} format, each requiring device-specific decryption keys stored in the application sandbox. Accessing these keys typically requires either root privileges on the device or the use of ADB (Android Debug Bridge) extraction methods, both of which require careful handling to preserve evidence integrity and maintain the forensic chain of custody \citep{kent2006}.

The Write Ahead Log (WAL) mechanism of SQLite adds a further dimension to forensic recovery possibilities. \citet{nishchal2024} document that SQLite databases, including WhatsApp's \texttt{msgstore.db}, maintain a WAL file that holds uncommitted or recently committed database pages. Forensic examination of the WAL file before it is checkpointed into the main database can reveal deleted messages, revoked content, and ghost records that are no longer visible in the primary database \citep{nishchal2024}. This technique has become a standard component of advanced WhatsApp forensic analysis, particularly for investigations involving the deletion or revocation of incriminating messages.

The cost and accessibility barriers of commercial forensic tools present a critical limitation for law enforcement agencies in lower-income contexts. Cellebrite UFED subscriptions can exceed USD~10,000 annually depending on licensing tiers and feature unlock credits, placing them beyond the reach of most African law enforcement agencies \citep{reddit2023}. This financial barrier has stimulated interest in open-source alternatives, though these require substantial technical expertise and consistent updates to remain effective as WhatsApp evolves its database schema and encryption formats.

\section{Local Perspective}

Malawi's digital landscape is characterised by a paradox of high mobile phone penetration and low digital literacy, creating conditions that are increasingly exploited by cybercriminals. \citet{kajoloweka2022} document the country's vulnerability to cyber-enabled crimes in this environment, noting that citizens are disproportionately exposed to online threats through popular platforms such as WhatsApp, which dominates mobile communication across all demographic groups in the country. Users, particularly those with low digital literacy, often contribute unintentionally by deleting critical messages out of fear or shame, falling for phishing scams, or failing to use secure communication methods, resulting in the loss or corruption of key digital evidence.

Criminals, on the other hand, exploit platform vulnerabilities including encryption gaps, fake account creation, message alteration or fabrication, and privacy features to conceal identities, thereby generating falsified evidence that undermines investigations \citep{vijayan2018}.

The scale of WhatsApp related cybercrime in Malawi has risen significantly in recent years. \citet{chiputula2024} reports a surge in mobile money fraud, impersonation through fake accounts, cyberbullying, and sextortion cases, with WhatsApp identified as the primary medium in the majority of incidents. \citet{nyirenda2024} notes that although awareness of cybercrime is growing, only a small proportion of victims report incidents to law enforcement, partly due to insufficient confidence in investigators' capacity to handle digital evidence competently. This underreporting perpetuates a cycle in which the true scale of WhatsApp-related cybercrime remains statistically obscured.

INTERPOL's 2025 report on cybercrime in Africa documents a sharp regional increase in WhatsApp facilitated crimes, including romance scams, investment fraud, and organised criminal coordination \citep{interpol2025}. Malawi is not immune to these regional trends, and the country's law enforcement agencies, most notably the Malawi Police Service, face compounded challenges in investigating such crimes. \citet{chimoyo2024} documents that the Malawi Police Service remains significantly under-equipped for digital forensic work, lacking comprehensive tools necessary for conducting thorough WhatsApp database investigations. The absence of a dedicated, locally appropriate forensic toolkit capable of lawfully accessing and analysing encrypted WhatsApp messages represents a critical operational gap.

A particularly stark illustration of the consequences of this gap is found in the high profile The National Oil Company of Malawi (NOCMA) fuel procurement case, in which WhatsApp evidence presented against the accused was successfully challenged by the defence on grounds of insufficient forensic rigour \citep{makossah2021}. The court assigned little to no evidentiary weight to the WhatsApp chats and recordings submitted, highlighting how even substantial digital evidence can be rendered inadmissible when the methods of extraction, preservation, and authentication do not meet established forensic standards. This case underscored the operational and legal urgency of developing forensically sound WhatsApp investigation capabilities within Malawi.

The legal framework for digital evidence in Malawi is provided primarily by the Electronic Transactions and Cyber Security Act No.\ 33 of 2016, which under Section 83 grants legal authority for the seizure and preservation of digital evidence. Sections 16(1) and 16(2) of the same Act establish that electronic records and messages are admissible in court, provided their integrity and authenticity can be demonstrated. However, \citet{rapozoND} notes that adherence to these standards is consistently hindered in practice by the absence of standardised forensic extraction tools, inadequate investigator training, and the lack of a clear national framework for digital evidence handling.

Cases such as Majiga (2021), Majawa (2023), and Joshua (2024) demonstrate that Malawian courts have admitted WhatsApp evidence under appropriate conditions, providing precedent for its use in criminal proceedings. However, the prevailing practice among investigators relying on screenshots and manual phone inspections rather than forensically sound database extraction produces evidence that is easily contested on grounds of authenticity, tamper evidence, and chain of custody \cite{burgess2025}. \citet{guttman2022} emphasises that digital forensic procedures must generate tamper-evident, cryptographically verifiable records to survive judicial scrutiny, a standard that current Malawian investigative practice frequently fails to meet.

The gendered dimensions of WhatsApp cybercrime in Malawi also warrant attention. \citet{interpol2025} notes that women, who represent a particularly active segment of WhatsApp users, are disproportionately targeted in sextortion, harassment, and impersonation cases. These crimes frequently involve the manipulation or fabrication of WhatsApp messages, placing further evidentiary demands on forensic tools to detect altered content and reconstruct authentic communication timelines. The toolkit proposed by this research must therefore address not only general forensic needs but also the specific evidence integrity challenges arising in gender-targeted cybercrime cases.

When a company seeks to investigate WhatsApp related policy violations involving its employees, it faces significant challenges due to the encrypted nature of the WhatsApp database and the deletion of suspects' messages.

This calls for tools dedicated to investigate such cases effectively and efficiently.

\section{Identified Gaps}

A systematic review of the existing literature reveals two principal gaps that directly motivate and define the scope of this research. These gaps represent both practical deficiencies in current forensic practice and theoretical limitations in the scholarly understanding of WhatsApp forensics in resource constrained contexts.

% ------ Subsection 2.5.1 -----------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Lack of an Integrated Toolkit Offering a Unified, Automated Framework for Handling WhatsApp Artefacts}

Investigators who conduct WhatsApp digital forensics analysis face various challenges due to the ever-changing architecture of the WhatsApp application, which includes the following.

% ------ Subsubsection 2.5.1.1 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Fragmented Open-Source Forensic Tooling}

The most significant structural deficiency in current WhatsApp forensic practice is the fragmentation of available open source tools. WhatsApp Viewer, Whapa, and WhatsApp Key/Database Extractor each address isolated components of the forensic workflow but do not form a coherent, interoperable pipeline. \citet{tusharanshu2023} document how this fragmentation forces investigators to manually orchestrate multiple tools, each with its own input/output format, dependencies, and failure modes. This manual chaining introduces unacceptable risks of evidence contamination, procedural error, and reproducibility failures all of which can undermine the admissibility of extracted evidence in court. No existing open source toolkit provides a unified, automated framework that spans the complete WhatsApp forensic workflow from acquisition through decryption, parsing, and court ready reporting.

% ------ Subsubsection 2.5.1.2 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Evolving Encryption Formats and Schema Variability}

WhatsApp has introduced successive backup encryption formats crypt12, crypt14, and crypt15 with meaningful differences between WhatsApp Messenger and WhatsApp Business. \citet{nishchal2024} notes that existing forensic tools frequently support only a subset of these formats, requiring investigators to use manual workarounds such as application downgrading to access evidence on devices running newer WhatsApp versions. \emph{Additionally, the internal database schema of \texttt{msgstore.db} has evolved across WhatsApp updates, introducing new tables and column structures most notably the addition of the @lid Business identifier system that have not been comprehensively documented or supported by existing forensic tools. This schema variability means that SQL queries and parsing scripts developed for one WhatsApp version may produce incomplete or erroneous results against a different version.}

% ------ Subsubsection 2.5.1.3 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Elevated Privilege Requirements and Evidence Integrity}

Accessing WhatsApp's encrypted database files and device-specific decryption keys typically requires either root privileges on the Android device or full logical acquisition through ADB. Conventional full-device imaging approaches capture all device data indiscriminately, generating volumes of forensically irrelevant information and consuming significant storage and processing resources. \citet{aggarwal2019} argue that this broad approach is misaligned with modern investigative needs, where investigators require the ability to perform sparse, targeted acquisition focused on specific application artefacts. Furthermore, existing key extraction methods that rely on application downgrading directly interact with the target device's data and carry a risk of altering the very evidence being sought, potentially violating chain of custody requirements.

% ------ Subsection 2.5.2 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Lack of a WhatsApp Application-Level Evidence Acquisition Tool}

Investigators in Malawi face significant challenges in acquiring evidence directly from WhatsApp due to the absence of a specialised application-level tool \citep{chimoyo2024, mtegha2024}.

% ------ Subsubsection 2.5.2.1 -----------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Manual and Error-Prone Evidence Collection}

In cases where access to specialised tools is absent, investigators resort to screenshots, message exports, or multiple partial tools. This increases the risk of evidence loss, data corruption, or inadmissibility in court, since these sources can be altered or modified \citep{nishchal2024}.

% ------ Subsubsection 2.5.2.2 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Dependence on General Forensic Tools}

Most investigators rely largely on general mobile forensic tools, such as Cellebrite, Oxygen, and MOBILedit. These tools are designed for full-device extraction rather than WhatsApp-specific evidence, and therefore cannot access encrypted WhatsApp databases, call logs, media, or metadata in a reliable way, which leaves critical WhatsApp evidence incomplete or inaccessible \citep{alissa2019}. This dependence significantly undermines the efficiency, accuracy, and legal defensibility of forensic investigations. This highlights the urgent need for a dedicated application-level acquisition tool that can securely and systematically extract WhatsApp artefacts in a form suitable for analysis and courtroom presentation.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter examined existing relevant literature on digital forensics, WhatsApp forensic analysis, and mobile cybercrime investigations, with specific attention to the Malawian context. The review identified critical gaps in current forensic tooling, particularly the lack of a unified, automated open-source framework for WhatsApp evidence acquisition, decryption, and analysis. In the next chapter, the system design and architecture of the proposed unified WhatsApp forensic toolkit are presented to address the gaps identified in this review.

\section{Introduction}

This chapter outlines how the WhatsApp digital forensic toolkit was designed to come up with a working forensic toolkit that can be used in forensics. The system adopts a modular forensic architecture designed to support acquisition, analysis and reporting of WhatsApp artefacts. Each module performs a specific function while maintaining integrity and legal compliance. The system integrates multiple open-source tools to come up with a unified toolkit running on a portable Ubuntu OS.

% ------ Section 3.2 ---------------------------------------------------
\section{Target User Categories}

The unified forensic toolkit has been designed to serve three user categories: law enforcement investigators as primary users, judiciary and courts as secondary users, and private sectors as tertiary users. These categories are strongly involved in the digital forensics process.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Law enforcement investigators:} These are police and investigators who are responsible for working on mobile money fraud, impersonation, cyberbullying, sextortion and other cybercrimes. The toolkit is designed to enable law enforcers to perform data acquisition from the suspect's device, conduct data analysis and generate reports that can be used in a court of law. The toolkit also helps to maintain chain of custody and the integrity of the evidence.

    \item \textbf{Judiciary and courts:} These are legal and judicial personnel needing to verify the integrity of evidence in legal proceedings. The toolkit is designed with integrity checks including chain of custody that helps with admissibility of evidence in a court of law. Judicial personnel are able to trace whether the evidence has been tampered with or not.

    \item \textbf{Private sector:} These are tertiary users such as corporate security teams investigating employee policy violations, IT auditors conducting internal investigations, and forensic service providers offering WhatsApp evidence extraction services. The toolkit is designed to allow data acquisition, analysis and report generation for WhatsApp artefacts.
\end{enumerate}

% ------ Subsection 3.2.1 ----------------------------------------------
\subsection{System Requirements Overview}

The goal was to develop a WhatsApp forensic toolkit that allows digital forensic experts to conduct WhatsApp forensic investigations by using a tool that is tailored for WhatsApp-based crimes, eliminating the need to adapt general-purpose forensic toolkits. The requirements of the system are grouped into two categories: functional requirements and non-functional requirements.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.2.1.1 -----------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Functional Requirements}

Functional requirements highlight the key capabilities of the WhatsApp forensic toolkit that enable it to achieve user expectations.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Case management}

    The system shall allow investigators to create a new workspace that includes:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Auto-generated unique case IDs.
        \item Registration of the investigator's credentials.
        \item Capture and storage of an authorised warrant number.
        \item Automatic creation of a full directory scaffolding (nested folders for evidence, logs, cryptographic hashes and reports).
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Evidence acquisition}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The system should support sparse acquisition of WhatsApp databases (\texttt{msgstore.db.crypt*}), media files, contact databases and call logs.
        \item The system must provide acquisition methods including ADB (Android Debug Bridge).
        \item The system should preserve original evidence by operating in a read-only mode using write-blocking capabilities \citep{kent2006, aggarwal2019}.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Integrity verification}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item At the beginning of the analysis session, the system shall recalculate cryptographic hashes of all the evidence acquired and compare them against hashes generated at acquisition. The system shall automatically terminate analysis if the hashes do not match, thereby preventing analysis of potentially compromised evidence \citep{guttman2022}.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Forensic analysis}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The system should parse decrypted databases to extract and display:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Chat messages with timestamps.
            \item Contact information and phone numbers.
            \item Call logs.
            \item Media file references.
            \item Group metadata.
            \item Profile information (display messages, status messages, profile references).
        \end{itemize}
        \item The system must provide timeline visualisation of chat activities to assist investigators in understanding event sequence.
        \item The system should allow filtering of evidence by date range, participant or message type.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Report generation}

    Investigators can generate reports in several formats including Google Material Design, HTML, CSV reports, plain-text transcripts and PDF documents. Regardless of the report format used, the toolkit automatically generates chain of custody metadata and investigator credentials directly in each report.

    \item \textbf{Audit logging}

    All investigator actions are recorded in three separate logs: an activity log specific to each case, a dedicated chain of custody log, and a global audit log that tracks cross-case activity with full session information.
\end{enumerate}

% ------ Subsubsection 3.2.1.2 -----------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Non-Functional Requirements}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Performance}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit must perform operations within a timeframe suitable for real-world investigators. This is to ensure investigators are not delayed by slow processing during active cases.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Usability}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit must be accessible to investigators of different levels of digital forensics expertise.
        \item The toolkit must include a quick-start mode that executes the complete forensic pipeline with minimal user input.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Security and integrity}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit must ensure that extracted evidence is not tampered with, is verifiable, and is legally admissible throughout the entire forensic workflow, as required by the Electronic Transactions and Cyber Security Act (2016) of Malawi and ACPO Good Practice Guidelines \citep{guttman2022}.
        \item All required evidence files (databases, media, configuration files) must be automatically set to read-only mode (\texttt{chmod 444}) immediately after acquisition.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Portability}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit should function completely offline. There should be no need for an internet connection during acquisition or reporting.
        \item The toolkit should work on Linux distributions that have Bash 5.x, SQLite3, and standard GNU capabilities.
        \item The toolkit must support bootable forensic environments for investigators operating in the field.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Resource efficiency}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit should not consume CPU resources when idle.
        \item The toolkit should not require any network resources.
        \item The toolkit should operate on limited hardware, considering that investigative environments in Malawi may lack high-end workstations \citep{chimoyo2024}.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Legal and compliance}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The toolkit must comply with all four ACPO Good Practice Guidelines:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Principle 1 (No alteration): Enforced through \texttt{chmod 444} and \texttt{sqlite3} read-only mode.
            \item Principle 2 (Competent handling): Enforced through investigator credentialing before any operations.
            \item Principle 3 (Audit trail): Enforced through \texttt{activity.log}, \texttt{chain\_of\_custody.log} and \texttt{global\_audit.log}.
            \item Principle 4 (Accountability): Enforced through session ID and investigator attribution.
        \end{itemize}
        \item The toolkit should maintain a global audit log that records actions across all cases.
        \item All log entries should be timestamped, append-only and non-editable through normal toolkit operations.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\section{General System Design}

This section presents the essential general system designs of the WhatsApp forensic toolkit that transform theoretical requirements into a concrete plan, ensuring a clear path to successful implementation of the functional and non-functional requirements. The following subsections explain the architecture design, which defines the high-level layers (acquisition, analysis and reporting), the software design, which explains the behavioural logic of each module, and the program design, which details the database structure, schema and algorithms necessary for implementation.

% ------ Subsection 3.3.1 ----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Software Architecture Design}

This subsection defines the architecture design of the forensic toolkit, establishing the structural organisation of components, their interactions and deployment considerations. The toolkit is designed to prioritise portability, integrity and ease of use for investigators operating in resource constrained environments such as Malawi \citep{chimoyo2024, mtegha2024}. The toolkit follows a modular shell library architecture, meaning work is divided among small, independent modules where each handles one specific task, with a single entry-point orchestrator that controls the workflow of specialised library modules by invoking them in a defined order.

% ------ Subsection 3.3.1 - Module Descriptions ----------------
The toolkit consists of seven modules, each performing a distinct forensic function within the unified pipeline.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Wa\_forensic module:} This is the orchestrator which, in the entry point, starts the toolkit and loads all four modules so they are ready for use and calls the right module at the right time. The orchestrator is also responsible for error handling and user interaction by displaying simple menus which prompt the investigator to enter information such as case number and warrant reference.

    \item \textbf{Case\_manager module:} This module is responsible for creating a workspace for a case. When a case is created, it generates an auto-ID which is unique for each case. It also loads any existing cases, which is important when an investigator is resuming work on an investigation that was restarted earlier, and lists all available cases displaying key information such as case ID, title, creation date, current state, and name of the assigned investigator. The \texttt{case\_manager} also saves case states at different stages of investigation, recording whether an investigation is new, in acquisition, analysis, reporting, or archive. It also deletes cases where necessary and displays detailed case information.

    \item \textbf{Data\_acquisition module:} This module handles extraction of WhatsApp data from an Android emulator using Android Debug Bridge (ADB). ADB establishes the connection and access between the forensic workstation and the emulator. Once connected, the module initiates a root shell, which grants unrestricted access to protected directories. The module issues copy commands to move database files from the protected area to the emulator's shared storage, which is accessible to the host computer. Throughout the acquisition process, the module simultaneously computes SHA-256 and MD5 hashes of each file being transferred and applies write protection to render the files read-only.

    \item \textbf{Integrity\_validation module:} This module verifies that all acquired evidence remains unaltered before any analysis proceeds. It checks that every file within the evidence directory has its read-only attribute set correctly. The module performs rehashing to ensure that no artefact has been corrupted or tampered with during storage by implementing a strict cryptographic verification workflow. If a single file mismatches any computed hash, the module issues a blocking exception that prevents the forensic toolkit from proceeding to the analysis phase.

    \item \textbf{Db\_handler module:} This module is responsible for all operations involving the WhatsApp database. Firstly, it validates the database to ensure that it is genuinely a WhatsApp database and has not been tampered with. Secondly, the module acquires the database by connecting to the Android device using ADB and navigating to the WhatsApp sandbox where relevant artefacts are stored. Thirdly, it auto-discovers the database by detecting which version of the encrypted database is present on the device and identifies the appropriate decryption tools for that format, without requiring manual input from the investigator. Once decryption is complete, the module exports all tables in their raw formats. Lastly, the module supports custom SQL commands, allowing experienced investigators to execute specific queries against the decrypted databases.

    \item \textbf{Chat\_analyser module:} This module is responsible for analysing the decrypted WhatsApp database and extracting meaningful information from it. WhatsApp stores all messages in a table typically known as \texttt{messages}. The module detects and automatically locates this table and extracts all messages along with their associated fields. The module also detects the chat table, which contains metadata about each conversation thread, and the JID table, which stores a unique identifier for each contact and group. The module performs Q1 through Q8 forensic analysis queries eight predefined queries that extract the most valuable evidence in WhatsApp cybercrime investigations; for example, Q1 extracts messages containing money-related keywords and Q2 extracts all deleted messages. The module generates statistics that provide a quick overview of the WhatsApp dataset and produces a per-chat HTML deep-dive report.

    \item \textbf{Report\_generator module:} This module is responsible for producing all final reports from the forensic investigation. It generates an HTML report, which is a web-based report viewable in any browser and is useful for internal reviews and supervision briefings. The module produces PDF reports, which are generally used for court submission. It also produces CSV reports in a spreadsheet-friendly format, useful for investigators who want to perform additional analysis using spreadsheet software. Additionally, the module generates plain-text transcripts, which are more useful for quick review on any device. Lastly, the module can generate all report formats simultaneously instead of generating each separately, saving time and ensuring consistency across all reports.
\end{enumerate}

% ------ Subsection 3.3.2 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Program Design}

The Unified WhatsApp Forensic Toolkit was designed as a modular, component-based solution consisting of an orchestrator entry point, four library modules, and two standalone scripts. Together, these components form a cohesive forensic investigation workflow, from case creation through evidence acquisition to report generation.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.1 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Orchestrator Component}

This component was designed to serve as the main entry point and central controller for the entire forensic toolkit. It initialises the environment, manages the user interface, and coordinates the other modules. The functions of this component are as follows.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Session Initialisation:} Generates a \texttt{session\_ID} that links all actions performed during an investigation session.
    \item \textbf{Dependency Checking:} Verifies that all required tools are present, including \texttt{sqlite3}, \texttt{sha256sum}, \texttt{md5sum}, \texttt{adb}, and Python 3.
    \item \textbf{Menu Management:} Manages the interactive menu loop using \texttt{validate\_menu\_input()} and input validation to prevent injection attacks.
    \item \textbf{Workflow Orchestration:} Coordinates the complete forensic workflow in a strict, defined order.
    \item \textbf{Logging:} Provides the universal function \texttt{log\_action()} that appends to both the per-case logs and the global audit log simultaneously.
\end{itemize}

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.2 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Case Management Component}

This component was designed to manage the complete lifecycle of forensic cases from creation through loading, saving, and deletion. It ensures that each case is properly isolated and that evidence integrity is maintained. This component is divided into four modules: the case creation module, the case loading module, the case deletion module, and the state persistence module.

\textbf{Case Creation Module.} This module handles the creation of a new forensic case. Its inputs include investigator name, badge ID, organisation, warrant number, suspect phone number, and device details. The module validates all user inputs such as phone number format and required fields and auto-generates a unique Case-ID to ensure each case is properly isolated. It then creates the complete directory structure. The outputs of this module are a case directory with an ACPO compliance record, and empty subdirectories for databases, reports, logs, and evidence.

\textbf{Case Loading Module.} This module handles resuming work on an existing case. Its input is the selection of an existing case from a list. The module displays all cases with investigator names, restores the session state from the \texttt{.case\_state} file, and exports case variables to the environment. The output is a restored case environment ready for analysis.

\textbf{Case Deletion Module.} This module handles the removal of a case. Its inputs are the case selection and a confirmation prompt. The module requires explicit confirmation before removing the entire case directory and all associated evidence. The output is a confirmation message and an updated case list.

\textbf{State Persistence Module.} This module handles saving the current investigation state. Its input is the set of current case variables. The module writes all case variables to a \texttt{.case\_state} file as \texttt{export VAR=value} lines. The output is a \texttt{.case\_state} file that can be sourced to restore the session at any time.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.3 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Evidence Acquisition Component}

This is a standalone component that handles the physical extraction of WhatsApp data from a rooted Android emulator through ADB (Android Debug Bridge). It comprises four modules: the ADB connection module, the data acquisition module, the write protection module, and the hash generation module.

\textbf{ADB Connection Module.} This module establishes and validates the connection to the Android emulator. Its input is a connected, rooted Android emulator. The process validates the ADB connection using \texttt{adb wait-for-device}, checks boot completion via \texttt{getprop sys.boot\_completed}, and verifies root access with \texttt{su -c id}. The output is a confirmed ADB connection ready for acquisition.

\textbf{Data Acquisition Module.} This module handles the extraction of databases and media files. Its input is the target case directory path. The process copies WhatsApp data using \texttt{su -c 'cp -r /data/data/com.whatsapp/ /sdcard/'} and pulls the copied data using \texttt{adb pull}. It also pulls media from \texttt{/sdcard/Android/media/com.whatsapp/}. The outputs are acquired databases and media files stored in the case folder.

\textbf{Write Protection Module.} This module ensures that acquired evidence cannot be tampered with. Its input is the acquired evidence folder. The process applies \texttt{chmod -R -w} to all evidence directories immediately after the pull operation. The output is a set of read-only evidence files with permissions set to \texttt{chmod 444}.

\textbf{Hash Generation Module.} This module produces cryptographic records of the acquired evidence. Its inputs are the acquired evidence directories. The module computes SHA-256 hashes using \texttt{tar -c DIR | sha256sum} and writes the results to \texttt{hashes.txt} with a timestamp. The output is a cryptographic hash record of the evidence state at the time of acquisition.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.4 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Integrity Verification Component}

This standalone component provides cryptographic proof that evidence acquired has not been modified or tampered with between acquisition and analysis. It serves to prove that evidence is verifiable and that integrity has been maintained. It is broken down into four modules: the write protection verification module, the hash re-computation module, the tamper detection module, and the failure handling module.

\textbf{Write Protection Verification Module.} This module checks that the read-only mode of evidence files and directories has been maintained. Its input is the case folder path. The module checks that evidence directories hold \texttt{chmod 444} (read-only) permissions using the \texttt{stat} command. The output is a confirmation of write protection, or an error message if any file is found to be writeable.

\textbf{Hash Re-computation Module.} This module recomputes hash values to verify that evidence files remain identical to the originals acquired. Its inputs are the original hashes stored in \texttt{hashes.txt}. The module recomputes the SHA-256 hash of the evidence directories using the same \texttt{tar | sha256sum} method. The output is a hash comparison result, indicating either a match or a mismatch.

\textbf{Tamper Detection Module.} This module flags evidence that has been tampered with, based on the hash comparison result. Its input is the hash comparison result. If the hashes match, the module writes an \texttt{.integrity\_verified} flag file. If a mismatch is detected, it invokes the \texttt{handle\_failure()} function. The output is either a success state (\texttt{.integrity\_verified} flag set) or a failure state (analysis is blocked).

\textbf{Failure Handling Module.} This module defines the actions to be taken when an integrity check fails. Its inputs are the details of the failed integrity check. The module displays a forensic warning, logs the failure, and returns exit code 1. The outputs are a blocked analysis state and an option to re-acquire evidence.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.5 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Database Handler Component}

This component was designed to manage all interactions with WhatsApp SQLite databases in a forensically sound, read-only manner. It is divided into five subsystems: the database validation module, the database acquisition module, the query execution module, the custom SQL module, and the schema export module.

\textbf{Database Validation Module.} This module verifies that the required databases exist, are encrypted, and have not been modified. Its input is a database file path. The process checks for file existence and non-zero size, verifies SQLite magic bytes, and checks for a \texttt{.crypt*} extension indicating encryption. The output is a validation result: valid, invalid, corrupted, or encrypted.

\textbf{Database Acquisition Module.} This module copies databases into the case folder in a forensically sound manner. Its inputs are the source database path and the destination path. The process copies the database to the case folder, applies \texttt{chmod 444}, and computes both SHA-256 and MD5 hashes. The output is a read-only database copy accompanied by a hash record.

\textbf{Query Execution Module.} This module executes predefined SQL queries against the decrypted databases. Its inputs are an SQL query string and an output format specifier. The module always uses the \texttt{sqlite3 -readonly} flag, executes the query, and captures the output and row count. The outputs are query results in the specified format and a row count for audit logging.

\textbf{Custom SQL Module.} This module allows experienced investigators to run their own SQL queries. Its input is a custom SQL query from the investigator. The process validates that the query begins with \texttt{SELECT} and then executes it with the read-only flag. Outputs include query results for valid \texttt{SELECT} statements, or a rejection message for any non-\texttt{SELECT} statements.

\textbf{Schema Export Module.} This module extracts the database schema for forensic documentation. Its input is a database name. The process executes the \texttt{.schema} command to retrieve the full database structure. The output is an SQL schema suitable for inclusion in forensic reports.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.6 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Forensic Analysis Engine}

This is a standalone component that is the largest and most complex, containing the schema detection subsystem and eight forensic analysis modules (Q1--Q8).

\textbf{Schema Detection Subsystem.} This subsystem establishes connections within the databases. Its input is a database connection. The process inspects the actual database tables using \texttt{PRAGMA table\_info} and detects table names (e.g., \texttt{message} vs \texttt{messages}, \texttt{chat} vs \texttt{chats}, media table variants, and timestamp column variants). The output is detected schema information that is used to dynamically construct all subsequent queries.

\textbf{Activity Profiling Module (Q1).} This module categorises the message database based on timestamps. Its input is the message database. The process groups messages by hour-of-day and day-of-week using \texttt{strftime()} on Unix epoch timestamps. The output is a communication frequency heatmap identifying peak activity windows.

\textbf{Chat Reconstruction Module (Q2).} This module attempts to understand events by recreating conversation threads. Its inputs include the message database and a selected contact. The process joins the message table with the JID table to resolve anonymised identifiers to readable names and orders results by timestamp. The output is a complete conversation thread with timestamps and participant identification.

\textbf{Contact Mapping Module (Q3).} This module connects chats and messages to the corresponding entries in the contact database. Its inputs include \texttt{msgstore.db} and \texttt{wa.db}. The process cross-references JIDs from the messages database with the contacts database. The output is a mapping of JIDs to display names and stored contact numbers.

\textbf{Media Reconstruction Module (Q4).} This module was designed to understand the sequence of events using media found within chats. Its input is the message database. The process queries the \texttt{message\_media} or \texttt{media} tables. The output is a catalogue of shared files with type, filename, size, MIME type, and associated timestamps.

\textbf{Deleted Message Detection Module (Q5).} This module detects and flags any deleted messages within the chats. Its input is the message database. The process searches for messages with a \texttt{status = -1} or \texttt{deleted = 1} flag, or orphaned rows where the parent chat no longer exists. The output is a set of soft-deleted records with their original timestamps and content.

\textbf{URL Extraction Module (Q6).} This module extracts all URLs that were shared within the chats. Its input is the message database. The process queries the \texttt{message\_link} table or applies the regular expression pattern \texttt{http[s]?://[\textasciicircum\textbackslash s]+} to message text. The output is a list of all extracted hyperlinks with their associated message timestamps.

\textbf{Master Timeline Module (Q7).} This module constructs a single, complete chronological timeline of all activities from the beginning to the end of the dataset. Its input is the message database. The process performs a \texttt{UNION ALL} across messages, media events, and link events. The output is a unified chronological narrative of all digital activity.

\textbf{WAL Recovery Module (Q8).} This module attempts to retrieve uncommitted records from the Write-Ahead Log. Its input is the \texttt{msgstore.db-wal} file, if present. The process reads the WAL file and attempts to extract uncommitted records. The output is a set of messages recovered from WAL frames not yet checkpointed to the main database.

% ------ Subsubsection 3.3.2.7 ------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Report Generator Component}

This component produces court-admissible outputs in multiple formats, embedding all required forensic metadata. It is broken down into five modules: the HTML report module, the PDF report module, the CSV export module, the plain text module, and the per-chat deep dive module.

\textbf{HTML Report Module.} Its inputs include analysis results, case metadata, and evidence hashes. The module generates a Google Material Design HTML report with embedded chain-of-custody metadata, investigator credentials, and evidence hashes. All styles are inlined, meaning there are no external dependencies. The output is an HTML report saved to \texttt{reports/html/forensic\_report.html}.

\textbf{PDF Report Module.} Its input is the generated HTML report file. The module converts the HTML report to PDF using \texttt{wkhtmltopdf}. The output is a PDF report saved to \texttt{reports/pdf/forensic\_report.pdf}.

\textbf{CSV Export Module.} Its input is the raw query results. The module exports query output as comma-separated values. The output is a set of CSV files saved to \texttt{reports/csv/}, one for each query.

\textbf{Plain Text Report Module.} Its inputs are the analysis results. The module generates a plain-text version of the report suitable for viewing in any terminal or text editor. The output is a set of text files saved to \texttt{reports/text/}.

\textbf{Per-Chat Deep Dive Module.} Its input is the data for each individual chat. The module generates HTML transcripts with tabbed views covering messages, media, URLs, metadata, and chain of custody. The output is a set of per-chat HTML reports saved to \texttt{extracted/chats/}.

\textbf{Report Content Requirements.} Every report generated by the toolkit must embed the following elements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Case ID, investigator name, badge ID, organisation, and warrant number.
    \item Suspect phone number and device details.
    \item Session ID and report generation timestamp.
    \item Evidence file hashes (SHA-256 and MD5) sourced from \texttt{hash\_registry.txt}.
    \item An ACPO compliance statement.
    \item A chain-of-custody log excerpt.
\end{itemize}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter describes how the WhatsApp forensic toolkit was built. It is a command-line digital forensic solution developed for the investigation of WhatsApp communications on Android devices operating within an emulated environment. The toolkit is written primarily in Bash scripting, with SQLite used for all database queries and Python used for report formatting and PDF generation. These components work together to provide a complete investigation workflow that moves from evidence acquisition up to a court-ready report without requiring any third-party commercial software.

The development adopted the Association of Chief Police Officers (ACPO) Good Practice Guide for Digital Evidence \citep{acpo2012}, which defines four principles for handling digital evidence in a manner that ensures its admissibility in legal proceedings. These principles require that digital data is not changed, that access to original data is restricted to competent individuals, that a complete audit trail is maintained, and that the investigating officer takes overall responsibility for compliance with those principles. Every module and every log entry produced by the toolkit has been designed to satisfy at least one of these requirements.

In addition to the ACPO framework, the toolkit has been designed to comply with Malawi's Electronic Transactions and Cyber Security Act No.\ 33 of 2016 (ETCS Act). Section 16(1) of the ETCS Act confirms that an electronic record cannot be rejected as evidence because it exists in electronic form. Section 16(2) further requires courts to consider the reliability of the system that generated the record and the integrity of the information it contains. The toolkit directly addresses both requirements: reliability is enforced through procedural gating that prevents analysis from proceeding unless integrity verification has passed, and integrity is demonstrated through SHA-256 cryptographic hashing recorded in a persistent chain-of-custody log. The investigator's identity is taken at case creation and attached to every log entry. This satisfies the identification requirement. By embedding these requirements into the software architecture, the toolkit produces evidence that is to be ruled admissible than evidence collected through informal methods such as manual screenshots or unverified scripts.

% ------ Subsection 4.1.1 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Core Engineering Principles}

The implementation of this toolkit was governed by four engineering principles; every design decision made during implementation was guided by them:

\textbf{Forensic Integrity.} Evidence is preserved in its original state at all times. No operation, whether intentional or accidental, may modify the original data. This is enforced at the filesystem level through read-only permissions and file immutability flags, at the database access level through SQLite's read-only mode, and through exit-code gating that cancels the workflow if any phase fails.

\textbf{Standards Compliance.} Every module is designed to satisfy one or more ACPO principles. Compliance is included in the architecture of the toolkit rather than treated as a documentation exercise performed after development.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} Every operation is logged with a timestamp, the investigator's identity, the input parameters used, and the outcome. An independent examiner can reconstruct the exact sequence of forensic activities and verify the results independently, satisfying ACPO Principle 3.

\textbf{Accessibility.} The command-line interface uses numbered menus, input validation at every data-entry point, and descriptive error messages. These features are intended to accommodate investigators with varying levels of technical expertise without compromising forensic rigour.

% ------ Subsection 4.1.2 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Toolkit Workflow Overview}

The toolkit has a six-phase linear workflow. Each phase must complete successfully before the next may begin. This gate-based approach prevents unverified or potentially corrupted evidence from progressing into analysis or reporting, directly implementing ACPO Principle 1. The six phases are: Case Initialisation, Environment Setup, Evidence Acquisition, Integrity Verification, Forensic Analysis, and Report Generation. A failure at any phase causes the toolkit to exit with a non-zero status code and display a clear error message, blocking all subsequent phases until the failure is resolved. This procedural gating is implemented in \texttt{wa-forensics.sh} through conditional checks on the exit codes returned by each module.

\subsection{Justification of Bash as Primary Implementation Language}

GNU Bash was chosen as the main programming language for the WhatsApp forensic toolkit for Android because it works well in forensic workstation environments and is simple to use on Linux systems.

\textbf{Zero-dependency deployment.}
Bash comes already installed on most Linux systems used in digital forensics, such as Kali Linux, CAINE, and SIFT Workstation. Because the toolkit was fully written in Bash, there was no need to install extra software, interpreters, or runtime packages. This is very important in secure forensic environments where internet access may not be available or installing new software may not be technically allowed.

\textbf{Direct filesystem access.}
Bash allows direct interaction with the Linux filesystem. For example, when the \texttt{acquisition.sh} script runs the \texttt{adb pull} command, data is copied directly from the Android device to the computer storage. This reduces the chances of data corruption.

\textbf{Deterministic process control.}
Bash gives good control over how commands are executed. The toolkit checks the result of every important command to make sure each operation is completed successfully. This helps prevent failed or incomplete processes from affecting the integrity of the evidence.

\textbf{Audit trail generation.}
Bash includes built-in tracing features such as \texttt{set -x}, which can record every command that is executed and its result. These records help create an activity log that documents all actions performed during the forensic process, supporting proper evidence handling procedures.

\textbf{Courtroom transparency.}
Bash scripts are easy to read and understand. During legal proceedings, an expert can show the script code in court and explain how the toolkit works. There are no hidden compiled files or complex background processes, making it easier for others to verify and understand the forensic procedures used.

% ------ Subsection 4.2.3 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Role of Python in the Toolkit}

Python was used in a limited and controlled manner within the WA-Forensics Toolkit. Its role was restricted to report generation and schema variation detection, while all core forensic operations were performed entirely using Bash scripts.

For report generation, Python was used to format extracted findings and compile them into structured HTML and PDF reports. Python's stronger text-processing and formatting capabilities made it suitable for creating readable and well-organised forensic reports without affecting the main acquisition and extraction workflow.

All major forensic processes, including database querying, artefact extraction, evidence handling, and data processing, were implemented fully in Bash. This design preserved the lightweight and zero-dependency nature of the toolkit, ensured direct interaction with the Linux filesystem, and reduced reliance on additional software environments during forensic investigations. By limiting Python to reporting and schema identification tasks only, the toolkit maintained a simple and transparent forensic workflow while still benefiting from Python's advanced formatting and parsing capabilities.

% ------ Subsection 4.2.4 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Justification of SQLite for Database Interrogation}

SQLite was selected as the database interrogation engine because WhatsApp stores its data directly in SQLite database files such as \texttt{msgstore.db} and \texttt{wa.db}. Using SQLite allowed the toolkit to examine the databases in their original format without converting the data into another form. This helped reduce the chances of errors or accidental modification of evidence during the forensic process. SQLite also supports read-only and immutable access modes, which provided an extra level of protection by ensuring that the database files could not be changed during analysis.

Another reason for selecting SQLite was its built-in integrity checking feature called \texttt{PRAGMA integrity\_check}. This feature allowed the toolkit to verify the internal structure of the database and detect issues such as corrupted pages, damaged indexes, or incomplete records that normal hash verification may not detect. By using SQLite directly, the toolkit was able to perform reliable and transparent examination of WhatsApp data while maintaining the integrity of the evidence throughout the investigation process.

\section{Phase 1: Case Initialisation}

Investigation begins with case initialisation, managed by the \texttt{case\_manager.sh} module. When a new case is created, the toolkit assigns a unique case identifier in the format \texttt{CASE-YYYYMMDD-HHMMSS}, generated from the creation timestamp. The investigator may accept this identifier or use a custom one subject to format validation. The module creates a directory structure within the case's root folder. This structure separates original evidence from operational data at the filesystem level, which is a critical design decision for forensic integrity.

The separation of \texttt{evidence/} and \texttt{operations/} enables the toolkit to lock only the \texttt{evidence/} folder after acquisition, preventing any modification of the original artefacts. The \texttt{operations/} folder must remain writable so that logs, hash records, and reports can be written as the investigation progresses. Locking the entire case folder would prevent the toolkit from recording its own activity, which would break the chain-of-custody log and cause the toolkit to block the case by flagging changes in hash values.

The module prompts the investigator to enter their name, badge number, organisation, warrant number, and a brief case description. These details are saved to a \texttt{case\_info.txt} file within the operations folder and are also attached in the session header that appears at the top of every subsequent menu screen. \section{Phase 2: Environment Setup - Emulator and Device Linking}

Before evidence acquisition can happen, the forensic environment must be set up. This phase, implemented within the controller \texttt{wa-forensics.sh}, involves initialising an Android Virtual Device (AVD) and linking the evidence device's WhatsApp account to the emulated environment using WhatsApp's Linked Devices feature.

The emulator is required because the toolkit acquires WhatsApp databases from the evidence device through a controlled, isolated emulated environment. The AVD ensures that no live WhatsApp traffic can alter the evidence after acquisition. The linking process verifies that the WhatsApp data format on the emulator is compatible with that of the original evidence device, confirming schema compatibility before an acquisition attempt.

Specifically, this phase performs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Android emulator initialisation and boot-readiness check
    \item WhatsApp application version verification and compatibility matching between the evidence device and the emulator
    \item WhatsApp account linking via the Linked Devices QR code feature
    \item Data format match validation to confirm that the acquired database schema aligns with the emulator's WhatsApp installation
\end{itemize}

The toolkit in \texttt{Acquisition.sh} uses the ADB command \texttt{adb devices} to confirm that an emulator is connected and then polls the \texttt{sys.boot\_completed} system property in a loop until the device reports a fully booted state. This ensures that acquisition attempts are not made against an emulator that has not yet mounted the required data partitions.

% ------ Section 4.5 ---------------------------------------------------
\section{Phase 3: Evidence Acquisition Module}

The Evidence Acquisition Module establishes communication with the evidence device, validates its readiness, extracts WhatsApp artefacts, and applies initial write protection. This is implemented in \texttt{Acquisition.sh} using a structured sequence of ADB commands.

% ------ Subsection 4.5.1 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Targeted Acquisition Strategy}

A fundamental design decision in the WA-Forensics Toolkit is the adoption of a targeted acquisition approach rather than full device imaging, which can range from 64\,GB to 256\,GB on a modern Android smartphone and require many hours to complete. The toolkit extracts only the WhatsApp-relevant data paths, which reduces acquisition time from hours to minutes and reduces the storage footprint of each case. This decision is justified by three operational considerations.

First, the scope of the investigation is to focus on WhatsApp communications, so acquiring non-WhatsApp data would introduce unnecessary material that is outside the investigation scope. Second, this strategy reduces the risk of exposing personal data unrelated to the investigation, which has implications under data protection principles. Third, a smaller evidence set is faster to hash, faster to verify, and produces more readable reports, all of which improve investigative efficiency and benefit investigators.

The acquisition targets the following WhatsApp data paths on the Android device:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{/sdcard/WhatsApp/Databases} -- primary message database files (\texttt{msgstore.db}, \texttt{wa.db})
    \item \texttt{/sdcard/WhatsApp/Media} -- shared images, videos, audio messages, documents, and voice notes
    \item \texttt{/sdcard/WhatsApp/Logs} -- application log files
    \item \texttt{/sdcard/WhatsApp/Backups} -- local WhatsApp backup archives
    \item \texttt{/data/data/com.whatsapp/databases} -- internal database storage
    \item \texttt{/data/data/com.whatsapp/shared\_prefs} -- application preference files
\end{itemize}

This is because the \texttt{adb pull} command cannot read directly from protected application directories without root access. \section{Integrity Verification \& Preservation Module}

The integrity verification module ensures that the acquired WhatsApp evidence remains protected from any form of alteration so as to ensure that the evidence is admissible in a court of law. This module enforces ACPO Principle 1, which states that data stored in a storage medium must not be altered or changed, as that data may later be presented in court. To achieve this, it implements multi-level cryptographic verification to confirm that the acquired evidence has not been altered since acquisition. It then produces a verifiable record of this confirmation that persists throughout the investigation process.

The module is automatically called by the toolkit after acquisition. If verification fails, the case is immediately blocked and no analysis is permitted on that case. This implementation ensures that investigators cannot proceed with compromised evidence. Figure~\ref{fig:integrity_fail} shows the toolkit blocking a case following an integrity failure.

\subsection{Hierarchical Hashing Strategy}

The module implements a two-level hierarchical hashing strategy. Through this design, verification efficiency is balanced with granularity of fault isolation; for instance, if the master case folder hash fails verification, investigators can immediately descend to the individual evidence folder hashes to identify exactly which file was altered.

\textbf{Level 1 : Evidence folders hashing.} The evidence folders, WhatsApp app data and WhatsApp media data, are hashed individually using SHA-256 and the hash values are recorded in a structured hash record file stored in \texttt{hash\_registry.txt} in the operations folder within the case folder.

\textbf{Level 2 : Case folder hashing.} After hashing the evidence folders, the master case folder is also hashed using SHA-256 to ensure nothing is added or removed from the evidence folder and that it contains all its related data in its original form.

During the verification process, the toolkit recalculates the hash values using the same algorithm, SHA-256, and then compares the hash values with those calculated earlier and stored in the \texttt{hashes.txt} file. The integrity verification module also checks whether write protection was enforced on the evidence folders after acquisition. If the hash values match and all verification passes, analysis may continue; if they fail, the case is blocked and no analysis is performed. This ensures the toolkit presents court-admissible, forensically sound evidence that is free from all tampering.

\subsection{SQLite Database Structural Integrity Verification}

Beyond filesystem-level hashing, WhatsApp databases require database-level structural verification. A database file can have a matching SHA-256 hash yet contain internal corruption that renders queries unreliable or produces incorrect results. The integrity module addresses this by running SQLite's \texttt{PRAGMA integrity\_check} on each acquired database file. Figure~\ref{fig:integrity_check} shows the structural integrity check with read-only mode enforced.

The combination of SHA-256 file hashing and \texttt{PRAGMA integrity\_check} provides comprehensive evidence integrity assurance, ensuring that evidence is not only unmodified at the byte level but also structurally sound for querying. This high level of evidence integrity assurance cannot be achieved by either method alone, making this approach one that leaves the evidence strongly defensible and admissible in court.

\section{Phase 5: Forensic Analysis Module}

The Forensic Analysis module is the central intelligence component of the toolkit. It transforms raw WhatsApp database data from WhatsApp records into well-structured artefacts that investigators can interpret and use as evidence. The module was implemented using two Bash scripting files: \texttt{chat\_analyzer.sh} and \texttt{db\_handler.sh}. It executes structured SQLite queries against verified WhatsApp databases to extract required artefacts across six analytical dimensions.

The analysis module is designed to run after the evidence acquisition, write protection, hashing, and integrity validation phases have been completed successfully. There are two primary evidence databases analysed: \texttt{msgstore.db}, which contains all WhatsApp messages, group chats, media references, timestamps, and communication records; and \texttt{wa.db}, which contains contact information, WhatsApp account metadata, and saved display names.

The primary purpose of this module is to automate the extraction, interpretation, and classification of forensic artefacts from WhatsApp databases while maintaining forensic standards and compliance. All database operations are performed using SQLite's read-only mode and filesystem-level write protection. Figure~\ref{fig:sqlite_readonly} shows the SQLite engine flag enforcing read-only permission.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{assets/images/fig4.9.png}
    \caption{SQLite engine flag enforcing read-only permission}
    \label{fig:sqlite_readonly}
\end{figure}

This enforcement means that even if the filesystem-level write protection were to be weakened or misconfigured, SQLite would refuse to write to the evidence database. Only \texttt{SELECT} statements, \texttt{.schema}, and \texttt{.tables} meta-commands are permitted, while any attempt to execute \texttt{INSERT}, \texttt{UPDATE}, \texttt{DELETE}, \texttt{DROP}, \texttt{VACUUM}, or \texttt{REINDEX} is blocked. This approach aligns with digital forensic standards that require that original evidence should not be modified during the investigation process \citep{acpo2012, nist2014mobile}.

\subsection{Schema Detection and Adaptive Querying}

The WhatsApp database schemas vary across different versions. Research by \citet{lekhac2022} documented that table names, column names, and data types change between WhatsApp releases. The message table has been named both \texttt{message} and \texttt{messages} in different versions; some metadata columns present in older versions may be renamed, relocated, or removed in newer releases \citep{anglano2014}. These schema variations cause forensic tools to fail because of hard-coded SQLite queries that fail on databases from mismatched WhatsApp versions.

The toolkit addresses this through a runtime schema inspection approach implemented in \texttt{chat\_analyzer.sh}. Figure~\ref{fig:schema_detect} shows the discovery of the actual database schema during execution.

\subsection{Communication Profiling}

The Communication Profiling function identifies the relationship between the device owner and communicating parties, interaction behaviour, and dominant contacts in the WhatsApp evidence database. This enables investigators to identify which individuals play specific roles within the suspected communications and prioritise those conversations for deeper forensic examination. Figure~\ref{fig:comm_profiling} shows a sample communication profiling output extracted during toolkit testing.

The function groups messages according to contact identifiers (JIDs) and calculates communication metrics for each participant, such as the total number of exchanged messages, first communication timestamp, last communication timestamp, number of active communication days, and message distribution patterns. These metrics are calculated using aggregate SQLite functions.

The results help investigators identify contacts that communicate with the device owner; high-volume communication relationships indicate individuals of interest. If a suspect exchanges an unusually high number of messages with a specific contact while having minimal interaction with others, that contact becomes a priority of investigation. Results are exported to CSV and plain-text report files for review.

\subsection{Chat Reconstruction}

Investigators can reconstruct complete communication timelines between the device owner and specific WhatsApp contacts. This function converts raw database records into human-readable conversation sequences that investigators can review chronologically during forensic examinations.

Messages are extracted with their associated contacts. Each message is shown with direction, message type (text, media, or empty), and the conversation identifier from the WhatsApp message database. Messages are arranged according to their timestamps to reconstruct the exact sequence of communication events, enabling the investigator to conduct timeline analysis effectively and understand the communication as it occurred on the device.

The direction of each message is determined by analysing WhatsApp Jabber Identifiers (JIDs) and the \texttt{key\_from\_me} field. A message with \texttt{key\_from\_me = 1} is classified as outgoing communication sent by the device owner. A message with \texttt{key\_from\_me = 0} and the \texttt{@s.whatsapp.net} suffix in the sender field is classified as incoming from an external user. Group chat messages are identified by the \texttt{@g.us} suffix in the conversation identifier. This classification approach is documented by \citet{anglano2014} and \citet{lekhac2022book}. Depending on the database values present, messages may be categorised as text, image, video, audio or voice, document attachments, or contact details.

The final reconstructed conversation is exported to output files such as CSV and plain-text reports. Chat reconstruction is important because isolated messages lack contextual meaning when viewed independently. Reconstructing conversations in chronological order enables investigators to accurately interpret intent, response patterns, planning behaviour, and communication context.

% ------ Subsection 4.7.4 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Contact Mapping}

The WhatsApp application identifies users using WhatsApp Jabber Identifiers (JIDs), which appear as strings like \texttt{265888123456@s.whatsapp.net} \citep{lekhac2022book}. These strings are difficult for non-technical investigators to interpret without resolution. The toolkit resolves these identities into forms that investigators and courts can understand. For example, the string \texttt{795012973834586@s.whatsapp.net} becomes more meaningful when resolved to \emph{John Banda, +265888123456}, showing the actual telephone number and the saved name if it was saved in the device owner's phonebook.

This function can also reveal situations where a contact exists in communication records but has no corresponding saved name within the device contact directory. Unidentified contacts may become important investigative leads requiring further examination.

The exported results may support several investigative objectives, including identification of key communication partners, verification of saved identities, and detection of unknown or unsaved contacts.

\subsection{Media Analysis}

Media files shared in WhatsApp conversations are extracted, decoded, and classified, as they are among the most important artefacts referenced in the WhatsApp databases. Images, videos, voice notes, documents, stickers, and shared links carry critical evidence value in modern digital investigations \citep{anglano2014}.

Media files are extracted from the \texttt{msgstore.db} WhatsApp message database for all records containing media references. Each media record is analysed to determine its storage location and media type. The function extracts details including media file paths, file names, MIME types, WhatsApp media type codes, CDN URLs, thumbnail references, message timestamps, conversation identifiers, and sender or receiver contact information.

Each entry is classified as \texttt{LOCAL}, \texttt{CDN}, or \texttt{NO FILE} storage location, as documented by \citet{lekhac2022book}. Figure~\ref{fig:media_decision} illustrates the decision process used by this module to identify the storage location of media.

% ------ Subsection 4.7.6 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Deleted Message Detection}

The Deleted Message Detection function discovers WhatsApp messages that have been removed using WhatsApp's Delete for Everyone feature. Any attempt to erase evidence may indicate that a suspect is trying to hide information from investigators. The ability of this toolkit to detect deleted messages, even when their original content is not available, provides investigative insight.

When a message is deleted using the Delete for Everyone feature, WhatsApp does not physically remove the record but instead sets a deletion flag, replaces the original message content with a system placeholder, and retains metadata within the database \citep{anglano2014,lekhac2022book}.

The function queries WhatsApp database records for message deletion indicators to identify conversations in which message removal activity occurred. It also records information about who deleted the message. Even if the actual message content has been deleted, investigators can use the retained metadata to establish timeline reconstruction, pattern of evidence, distraction behaviour, and intent analysis. For example, a sudden increase in deletion activity before device seizure after a criminal incident may indicate deliberate attempts to conceal communication evidence.

The extracted deletion records and their metadata are exported into structured forensic reports for investigator review and court presentation.

% ------ Subsection 4.7.7 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{URL Extraction}

The URL Extraction function detects and extracts all hyperlinks shared in WhatsApp conversations. Hyperlinks carry investigative intelligence because they lead to external websites, cloud storage platforms, social media accounts, malware downloads, phishing infrastructure, or web-based communication resources associated with criminal activity. Research has confirmed that URLs shared through instant messaging applications like WhatsApp can provide valuable evidence and investigative leads during digital forensic examinations \citep{mahajan2013,anglano2014}.

Each URL is categorised by its platform, including YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Telegram, Google Drive, Dropbox, and WhatsApp CDN links. The function implements this capability by querying message content stored in \texttt{msgstore.db} and searching for web protocol prefixes such as \texttt{http://} or \texttt{https://}; messages containing these patterns are parsed to extract the actual hyperlinks from the conversation content.

Investigators may use this function to identify URLs used to conceal malicious destinations, cloud storage links used to transmit unauthorised documents, or external communication services employed to facilitate illegal activities. The extracted URLs and metadata are exported into CSV and text-based reports.

% ------ Subsection 4.7.8 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Database Handler}

The Database Handler module enforces protection and centralised database management across the toolkit. It ensures secure and strictly controlled database access across all analysis functions. Every analysis interaction with the WhatsApp evidence databases including Communication Profiling, Chat Reconstruction, Contact Mapping, Media Analysis, Deleted Message Detection, and URL Extraction is performed through this module. Centralised management provides integrity preservation throughout the analysis phase, prevents individual analysis modules from directly interacting with evidence databases in an uncontrolled manner, and assures uniform evidence protection procedures across the toolkit. This centralised evidence access approach is recommended in digital forensic research because it improves evidence consistency, auditability, and reliability during forensic analysis \citep{casey2011,carrier2005}.

Before executing any query, the database handler performs validation on the evidence database. It first confirms that the database file exists, its size is non-zero, and it is not currently writable. If any of these checks fails, the query is rejected and the failure is recorded in the audit log. Only a validated query is forwarded to the SQLite engine for execution.

The runtime schema detection and adaptive querying approach helps to address variations in WhatsApp database schemas across application versions. Studies have shown that WhatsApp frequently changes table names, column structures, metadata formats, and database relationships between releases, causing static forensic queries to fail when executed against unsupported versions \citep{anglano2014,lekhac2022book}. This approach enables the toolkit to adapt to multiple WhatsApp database versions without requiring manual modification of analysis queries, making it compatible with both older and modern WhatsApp releases.

Every database access event, query execution, schema detection process, validation check, and analysis transaction is recorded in the toolkit's audit logs. The logs include timestamps, executed functions, target database references, analysis stages, execution outcomes, and the identity of the person who performed each action. This logging enforces accountability by creating a verifiable audit trail documenting how evidence was accessed and analysed during the investigation process. Audit logging is an essential component of forensic transparency and chain-of-custody preservation, helping investigators, auditors, and courts to reconstruct the sequence of forensic activities performed on the evidence \citep{casey2011}.

\section{Phase 6: Reporting Module}

The Reporting Module constitutes the final operational phase of the toolkit and transforms all forensic findings into human-readable, professionally formatted, legally sound forensic reports. The module is implemented through \texttt{report\_generator.sh}, which gathers, organises, and processes the outputs generated by the toolkit.

Python libraries are used to generate professionally formatted PDF reports from the structured analysis findings and to improve report readability, consistency, and document presentation. Python is used exclusively for report formatting and PDF generation, while all forensic analysis, evidence acquisition, and database interpretation are implemented by the Bash scripting modules. Separation between analysis processing and reporting components is recommended in forensic software engineering because it improves maintainability, modularity, reproducibility, and evidence reliability \citep{casey2011,carrier2005}.

\subsection{Report Structure and Chain-of-Custody Integration}

Each generated report is an evidence document that includes all information necessary for independent verification. The report includes the following elements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case metadata:} Case identifier, case reference number, and legal authorisation document reference.
    \item \textbf{Investigator details:} Name, identifier, and report generation timestamp.
    \item \textbf{Evidence integrity section:} Master SHA-256 hash value, individual file hash values, number of files verified, and verification status.
    \item \textbf{Analytical findings:} All analytical outputs formatted for human review.
    \item \textbf{Chain of custody log:} A sequential record of every action performed on the evidence, including acquisition timestamp, integrity verification timestamp, analysis timestamp, and the identifying information of the investigator at each stage.
    \item \textbf{Report authenticity hash:} A SHA-256 hash of the report PDF itself, enabling verification that the report has not been altered after generation.
\end{itemize}

Including chain-of-custody data ensures that the report remains legally defensible even when separated from the case management system. Any independent third party can verify the cryptographic hashes against the evidence files and confirm the unbroken chain of custody from acquisition to reporting.

% ------ Subsection 4.8.3 -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Logging and Audit Trail}

The toolkit implements automated logging across all modules. Every action is recorded with all necessary details to enable complete reconstruction of the investigative process by an independent examiner, satisfying ACPO Principle 3.

Two log files are maintained:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{activity.log} (Technical operation record):} Contains commands executed, paths accessed, query results, warnings, and timestamps. Each entry follows a standardised format capturing timestamp, log level, originating module, and descriptive message.

    \item \textbf{\texttt{chain\_of\_custody.log} (Evidentiary handling record):} Captures the five Ws of evidence handling who (investigator identity), what (action performed), which (specific evidence item), when (timestamp), and outcome (success or failure status). This supports ACPO Principles 3 and 4 (accountability and reproducibility).
\end{itemize}

The chain-of-custody verification function in \texttt{wa-forensics.sh} confirms, at the conclusion of an investigation, that all six required events are present in the log: Evidence Acquired, Write Protection Applied, Hash Generated, Integrity Verified, Analysis Performed, and Report Generated. If any event is absent, the system reports the chain of custody as incomplete and flags the specific missing event for investigator attention before the report is finalised.

\section{Security and Forensic Integrity Mechanisms}

The toolkit implements defence-in-depth for evidence protection through multiple, independently operating security layers. No single point of failure can compromise forensic integrity. These layers include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Database layer:} SQLite read-only mode (\texttt{sqlite3 -readonly} with \texttt{mode=ro} URI) prevents any SQL write operations (\texttt{INSERT}, \texttt{UPDATE}, \texttt{DELETE}, \texttt{DROP}).
    \item \textbf{Permission layer:} Unix file permissions (\texttt{chmod 444}) prevent accidental modification by any user.
    \item \textbf{Filesystem layer:} Immutable attribute (\texttt{chattr +i}) prevents modification even by root without explicit \texttt{chattr -i}.
    \item \textbf{Procedural layer:} Exit code gating ensures the controller checks exit codes before advancing phases, preventing analysis of unverified or corrupted evidence.
    \item \textbf{Input layer:} Whitelist validation using regex pattern matching prevents command injection and path traversal attacks.
    \item \textbf{Execution layer:} Error trapping (\texttt{set -e} and \texttt{trap} handlers) prevents silent failures from going unnoticed and unlogged.
\end{itemize}

\section{Implementation Challenges and Resolutions}

Several implementation challenges were encountered during development. Each required a specific decision that influenced the final architecture of the toolkit.

\textbf{WhatsApp Database Schema Evolution.} WhatsApp changes its database structure between releases, causing static tools to fail when applied to databases from unsupported versions \citep{anglano2014,fayyad2022}. This challenge was resolved through the runtime schema detection approach described in Section~4.7.1.

\textbf{Root Access Variability.} Acquiring WhatsApp data from an Android device requires superuser (root) access to the \texttt{/data/data/com.whatsapp/} directory. The toolkit addresses this problem entirely by performing acquisition within a rooted Android Virtual Device (AVD), as explained in Section~4.4.

\textbf{Emulator-Device WhatsApp Version Parity.} WhatsApp installed on the emulator may not match the version on the evidence device. This was resolved by a version verification step during Environment Setup that validates compatibility before account linking continues.

% ------ Section 4.11 ---------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}

The current implementation has the following known limitations that investigators should be aware of before using this toolkit:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Deleted message recovery is not always possible; it depends on the presence of a SQLite WAL file containing pre-deletion data, which may not always be available after acquisition. The toolkit is only capable of detecting and flagging deleted records from the database. This limitation reflects the complexity of recovering deleted artefacts from WhatsApp databases and highlights the need for further comprehensive research in order to develop a recovery mechanism in future improvements of the toolkit.
\end{itemize}
